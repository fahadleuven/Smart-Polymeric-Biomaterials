Electrospinning a mechanocompatible external support for the Ross procedure

My research passion lies at the intersection of engineering, medicine, and materials science. I work within this scientific triangle by combining three pillars: (I) computer models and simulations in biomechanical engineering, (II) clinical applications in cardiac surgery, and (III) polymeric medical textiles for cardiovascular interventions.

Throughout my academic journey, I have focused on the development of a mechanocompatible external support for the Ross procedure. To achieve this, we use computational modeling to simulate the growth and remodeling of arterial tissue, and to predict long-term adaptation after surgery. As input for these models, we perform multiaxial mechanical testing to characterize the material behavior of cardiovascular soft tissues.

Through constitutive artificial neural networks, we then autonomously discover material models that can be incorporated directly into our simulations, increasing both accuracy and interpretability. Complementing the computational frameworks, electrospinning technology has enabled us to design a mechanocompatible and biodegradable polymeric textile, to serve as biodegradable external support around the artery. This textile has been successfully evaluated in a pre-clinical setting, demonstrating promising performance for future translational studies.

Schematic overview and research approach (see figure) to electrospin a mechanocompatible external support for the pulmonary autograft. (a) In the Ross procedure, a donor pulmonary artery is transplanted into the abdominal aortic position of the recipient. (b) Through a small animal model, unsupported pulmonary autografts were exposed to aortic blood pressures to mimic the surgical intervention. (c) Electrospun PCL:elastin scaffolds were used as polymeric external supports. (e) Upon material characterization, the electrospun tubular structures were optimized and implanted in-vivo. Postoperative μCT was used to characterize anatomical changes over time, followed by mechanical characterization and histological analysis of the tissues upon sacrifice. Finally, the biomechanics of the structures was assessed through infrared spectroscopy and mechanical ring-extensions after a degradation study.
